Konark Sun Temple

A monumental representation of the sun god Surya's chariot, built in the 13th century on the shores of the Bay of Bengal. The temple is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is renowned for its intricate stone carvings and architectural grandeur that showcase the Kalinga style of temple architecture.

Read on Wikipedia

History

The Konark Sun Temple was built in the 13th century by King Narasimhadeva I of the Eastern Ganga Dynasty. Legend has it that the temple was constructed in just 12 years with the help of 1,200 artisans. The temple's name is derived from the words 'Kona' (corner) and 'Arka' (sun), as it was built to worship the Sun God.

Architecture

The temple is designed as a colossal chariot of the Sun God, Surya, with twelve pairs of elaborately carved wheels pulled by seven horses. The temple symbolizes the passage of time, with the 12 pairs of wheels representing the 12 months of the year and the seven horses representing the seven days of the week. The walls are covered with exquisite stone carvings depicting mythological narratives, celestial beings, and daily life scenes.
